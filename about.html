About the Artist

Painting What Stays
After the Silence

I paint from the places I know by feel — the North Fork of the Flathead, the ridgelines above Hungry Horse Reservoir, the meadows and timber edges that thread through Glacier National Park. These landscapes are not backdrops. They are the subject.

My work begins in observation. Hours spent watching how fog lifts off a river at dawn, how a grizzly moves through beargrass without bending a stem, how a mountain holds light differently at every hour. I'm drawn to the moment just before something shifts — what I call the quiet edge. That threshold between stillness and motion, between seen and felt.

The paintings are built on value and atmosphere. I work loose, letting the paint carry weight where detail would only distract. A bear in a meadow isn't a portrait of an animal — it's the presence of something alive in a place that was wild long before any of us arrived. The mountains don't need explanation. They need to be felt.

I work primarily in oil, sometimes shifting to watercolor or ink for smaller studies done in the field. The oil paintings are where the real work happens — layered, considered, and anchored in the specific quality of light and air that defines this part of the West.

I live and paint in Montana. The land here is close, and it stays in the work.

— Marnie Henry
